PART 1

Local Hamiltonians and the Gibbs State

The basic model for quantum many-body systems

What is a quantum many-body system?

Imagine a grid of particles. Each one is a tiny magnet called a qubit. In real materials, nearby particles interact. Their spins influence each other. The Hamiltonian H is a giant matrix that encodes all these interactions. It works like an energy function for the system. Writing it down takes a matrix of size 2n by 2n. That is exponentially large in the number of particles n. For just 50 particles, the matrix is already too big to store on any classical computer.

But nature has a trick. The interactions are local. Each particle only talks to its neighbors. So the Hamiltonian can be written as a sum of local terms: H = E₁ + E₂ + ... + Em. Each Ea only touches a few nearby particles. This locality is what makes the problem tractable.

What is the Gibbs state?

At a given temperature T (or inverse temperature β = 1/T), the system settles into thermal equilibrium. Its state is the Gibbs state: ρβ = e-βH / tr(e-βH)

Think of it as the quantum version of a Boltzmann distribution. At high temperature (small β), the state is a chaotic soup. It is close to maximally mixed, meaning all configurations are equally likely. At low temperature (large β), the system freezes into ordered states. The lowest energy configurations dominate. This is the physics of phase transitions.

Each cell is a qubit. Color shows magnetization ⟨σz⟩. Warm colors mean spin up. Cool colors mean spin down. Arrows show spin direction. Gray lines are the interactions between neighbors.

What to watch

β small (hot). Spins point every which way. The state is nearly maximally mixed. All configurations are equally likely.

β large (cold). Spins align. The system goes through a phase transition into an ordered state. It works just like a magnet when you cool it below its Curie temperature.

This temperature driven competition between order and disorder is the key to everything that follows.

PART 2

Sudden Death of Entanglement

A universal law. Above a critical temperature, entanglement vanishes completely.

What is entanglement?

Entanglement is the quintessential quantum property. As Schrödinger put it: “I would not call that one, but rather the characteristic trait of quantum mechanics.”

A state is separable (not entangled) if it can be written as a mixture of product states: ρ = Σ pk ρkA ⊗ ρkB. In that case the correlations between particles are purely classical. Think of two coins that are correlated through a shared probability distribution.

If the state cannot be written this way, it is entangled. The particles share intrinsically quantum correlations. There is no classical analog.

The result (Theorem 1.3.1)

Take any local Hamiltonian. There is a critical inverse temperature βc. This critical temperature depends only on the geometry of the interactions (for example a 2D grid or a 3D lattice). It does not depend on the system size n.

Above βc (which means at high enough temperature), the Gibbs state is separable. It has exactly zero entanglement. The transition is sudden. As you cross βc, all thermal entanglement disappears.

This is striking because previous work could only prove separability at temperatures that were exponentially small in n. Everyone knew entanglement must die at some point. But the fact that it happens at a constant temperature regardless of how big the system is, that is a universal law.

Proof idea: The proof uses a quantum version of the sampling to counting reduction. This idea comes from classical algorithms for approximate counting. It decomposes the Gibbs state by sampling one particle at a time, factorizing it into a product of conditional states. If each conditional state is separable, the whole state is separable.

Phase diagram. The pink region (β below βc) is entangled. The blue region (β above βc) is separable. The critical line does not move as n grows.

Why this matters

Previous work could only bound proxy measures like mutual information or correlation functions. Those combine both classical and quantum correlations. This is the first result that directly controls entanglement itself, independent of classical correlations.

The theorem also proves that above βc, the Gibbs state can be prepared efficiently on a classical computer (Theorem 1.3.2). This means this regime offers no exponential quantum speedup for Gibbs sampling.

PART 3

Hamiltonian Learning at Any Temperature

Recovering a system's Hamiltonian from measurements of its Gibbs state.

The problem

You are given many copies of an unknown Gibbs state ρβ. You can measure local observables like tr(Xa ρβ) where Xa are Pauli operators on small groups of particles. From these measurements, you want to recover the Hamiltonian H that produced the state.

This is crucial for quantum device benchmarking. If someone builds a quantum simulator, you need to verify that it implements the right Hamiltonian. It is also fundamental physics. We want to discover what Hamiltonians nature uses.

The barrier at low temperature

Previous algorithms by Anshu and others (2020) and by Haah, Kothari and Tang (2022) only worked at high temperature (small β). At low temperature, all their tools fail.

  • High-temperature series expansion diverges.
  • Cluster expansion fails.
  • Markov property breaks down in quantum systems.
  • Partition function estimation becomes computationally hard.

The field believed low-temperature learning was intractable. Some even conjectured that low-temperature Gibbs states are pseudorandom. That would mean no efficient algorithm could learn from them at all.

Liu's breakthrough (Theorem 1.3.3)

The result gives the first efficient algorithm for Hamiltonian learning at any constant temperature. It runs in time polynomial in n and 1/ε.

Key ideas:
1. Instead of matching just local marginals (which leads to an intractable system), you measure a richer family of statistics. This uses a new polynomial approximation of the matrix exponential e-βH.
2. Formulate the estimation as a sum-of-squares (SoS) semidefinite program, which can be solved in polynomial time.
3. The SoS proof uses the fact that nested commutators of local terms are small. This is how locality tames the problem even at low temperature.

Algorithm intuition

1. Measure local observables tr(Xaρβ). Each gives partial information about the Hamiltonian coefficients.

2. Set up equations using a polynomial approximation to the matrix exponential. This creates a system that connects the measurements to the unknown parameters.

3. Solve via SoS. This is a semidefinite relaxation of the polynomial system. This is what makes the algorithm efficient.

PART 4

Quantum State Tomography and Certification

Learning and testing unknown quantum states from limited measurements.

The setting

You have an unknown quantum state ρ. This is a d by d density matrix, where d = 2n for n qubits. You can prepare many copies of ρ, measure them, and get classical outcomes. How many copies do you need to learn ρ to accuracy ε?

This is quantum state tomography. It is the most fundamental learning task in quantum information. It is the quantum equivalent of estimating a probability distribution from samples.

Key constraint: limited quantum hardware

The best tomography protocols by Haah and others (2017) and by O'Donnell and Wright (2017) use entangled measurements across all n copies at the same time. This requires quantum memory and multi-copy gates. Those are far beyond current devices.

The thesis studies two realistic settings.

  • Single-copy measurements: Measure one copy at a time, then discard it. No quantum memory is needed. This matches current hardware.
  • t-copy measurements: Measure t copies together (the device can store a few at a time). This interpolates between single-copy and fully entangled.

Results (Theorems 1.3.5 through 1.3.8)

Single-copy tomography: Θ(d³/ε²) copies This matches the upper bound of random measurements. Adaptivity does not help for trace distance.
Single-copy (fidelity): Θ̃(d³/γ) copies For infidelity γ, adaptivity does help. This is a rare separation between adaptive and nonadaptive.
t-Copy tomography: Θ̃(d³/(√t ε²)) Smooth tradeoff. More copies measured together means fewer total copies needed.
State certification: Ω(d³⁄²/ε²) Testing if ρ = σ (known) versus ρ ≠ σ. A tight lower bound that closes a gap.

Why lower bounds are hard

Classical lower bound techniques like Fano's inequality assume independent samples. In the quantum setting, the learner can choose measurements adaptively based on previous outcomes. Liu introduces a new framework.

Adaptive lower bound framework: Sample the unknown state ρ from a prior distribution. Then directly track the posterior after each measurement. Show that no matter what adaptive strategy the learner uses, the posterior remains too spread out to identify ρ accurately. This is a bare-handed approach that works even when classical information-theoretic tools fail.

The t-copy tradeoff (Theorem 1.3.8) is the first smooth interpolation between these extremes. It resolves an incompatibility between Schur sampling (a biased estimator that needs many copies at once) and single-copy averaging (unbiased but less informative).
